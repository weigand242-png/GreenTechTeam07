## Submission Guide

Here’s everything you need to know to hand in your project. Read it once, there are a couple of hard rules you don’t want to find out about at 13:59.

**Deadline: Friday, 29.05., 14:00 sharp.** One submission per team, no late entries.

Your team number (1–20) is in Discord. If you’re not sure which team you’re on, check your team channel.

---

### What you’re submitting

Three things, all bundled into one GitHub repo:

1. **A validated problem and customer pair** — who has the problem and how you know it’s real.
2. **A solution concept** with a clear value proposition.
3. **A tangible demonstration** — prototype, demo, mockup, simulation, or model. Something the jury can actually look at.

The jury scores you on problem validation, solution quality, prototype tangibility, business potential, and how you present at the expo. Each challenge may add one bonus criterion on top.

There is no stage pitch and no slide deck. On Friday afternoon you present your project live at your expo station while the jury circulates.

### Your repo: the rules

Everything lives in **one public GitHub repo per team.** A few things that aren’t negotiable:

- **GitHub only.** Not GitLab, not Bitbucket, not a shared Google Drive folder. If it’s not on [github.com](http://github.com), it doesn’t count.
- **Public**, so the jury can actually open it.
- **Created during Build Days.** We check the creation timestamp — pre-existing repos are out. The whole point is what you built here.
- **One repo per team.** Pick a teammate to host it on their account, make sure everyone has access.
- Use your team number in the repo name so we can find it — e.g. `team-07-projectname`.

---

### What goes inside

Your README is the jury’s landing page. Make it the first thing that’s clear. Include:

- Project name and a one-liner on what it does
- Your team number (1–20) and the names of everyone on the team
- The challenge / track you’re tackling
- The problem you’re solving and who has it — your validated problem and customer pair
- Your solution and value proposition, in plain language
- A link or pointer to your demo — prototype, mockup, simulation, whatever form it takes

Beyond the README, drop your actual code, prototype files, and anything else the jury might want to look at into the repo. The README is the table of contents — everything should be reachable from there.

### When the commits stop

Once 14:00 hits, **stop pushing.** No new commits until the expo wraps.

We check timestamps. Anything pushed after the deadline can disqualify the entry — the jury reviews what was submitted, not what got polished afterwards, and it has to be fair across all 20 teams.

If you spot a typo at 14:01, breathe and let it go. You built fast, the rough edges are part of the story. Once the expo is over the repo is yours again — push whatever you want.

### How you actually submit

We’ll post the submission link (Microsoft Forms) in Discord well before the deadline — keep an eye on the announcements.

When it’s live, you’ll fill in your team number, your repo URL, and a couple of confirmation fields. Submit early — you can edit the repo right up to 14:00, so there’s no reason to wait until the last minute.

### FAQ

**Can the repo be private?** No — has to be public.

**Can we use GitLab / Bitbucket instead?** No. GitHub only.

**Can we use code we wrote before the event?** Open-source libraries, frameworks, and starter templates are fine. Your own pre-existing project code is not.

**Multiple repos?** One main repo per team. Link extras from the README if you have them.

**API keys?** Never commit secrets. Use `.env.example` and add `.env` to `.gitignore`.

**What if we miss 14:00?** The form closes. No late entries.

**Can we keep working on the project after?** Yes — once the expo is done, push whatever you want.

## Challenges
The three challenges for GreenTech Build Days.

### Next Mobility Labs | Vehicle-to-Grid

Renewable energy has a timing problem. Solar peaks at noon and wind blows at night, but demand peaks in the evening when people come home, cook and plug in their cars. Green power gets wasted when there is a surplus, and fossil plants jump in when it runs short.

The fix is already parked in every company car park. Electric vehicles are mobile batteries, and they sit unused for an average of 22 hours a day. Vehicle-to-Grid turns them into part of the solution: instead of only consuming energy, they store it when it is green and cheap and feed it back when the grid needs it most.

The commercial platform that connects fleet operators to the energy system is still white space. How might we build it?

### Kenergy Solutions | The modern Energy Consultant

Most people want to live more sustainably at home and know they could cut their heating and electricity bills. But when they try to work out where to start, it gets too time-consuming and expensive. The advice online is either too generic or too complicated, and a rented flat in a 1970s building is a completely different case from an owner-occupied house built in 2005. The traditional route, hiring a certified energy consultant, is slow and expensive and for many people off-putting. So people who want to act don't. The knowledge gap is the bottleneck, not the willingness.

Imagine a platform that works like a knowledgeable friend. You tell it about your home: where you live, what kind of building it is, how you heat it, whether you own or rent. It tells you what you can do, what it will cost, what you will save and how to get started. Not generic tips, but a real plan built for your situation. It has to work for renters and owners alike, and for people with no technical background. How might we build it?

### SCHOTT | The Smart Opportunity Finder

SCHOTT makes specialty glass for MedTech, and its market intelligence team has to see where the market is heading before it gets there, so the right materials and capacity are ready when demand arrives. Spot an opportunity three years too late and you build capacity into a saturated market. Spot it three years too early and you invest in demand that does not exist yet. The narrow window where an opportunity is real but not yet obvious to everyone is where the advantage lives.

The signals are scattered across patent filings, clinical trial registries, funding rounds, regulatory filings and academic papers, and today most of that work is done by hand. Turn those public signals into a short, ranked set of concrete opportunities, each matched to one of SCHOTT's capabilities and backed by traceable evidence. How might we build it?
